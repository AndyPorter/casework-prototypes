Restrictive covenants 1
What are restrictive covenants?

A “restrictive covenant” is a promise by one person to another, (such as a buyer of land and a seller) not to do certain things with the land or property.

A restrictive covenant continues even when the current owner sells it to another person. Restrictive covenants continue to have effect even though they may have been made many years ago and appear to be obsolete.

Some common examples:

  • Not to keep a caravan on the property
  • Not to run a business from the property
  • Not to keep any animals other than domestic pets on the property

A Deed dated 12 February 1991 made between Helen Cassidy and Clara Teoh contains restrictive covenants.

Easements 1
What are easements?

“Easements” are rights to do or use something that the owner of one piece of land or property has over another piece of land or property owned by someone else.

One piece of land or property has the “benefit” and the other has the “burden” of the rights. In many cases, both pieces of land or property can have both benefits and burdens.

An easement attaches to the land or property itself and is not a personal right. Benefit and burden remain upon the affected land or property when there is a change of ownership.

Some common examples:

  • Rights of way such as public footpaths
  • Rights to run service pipes and cables underground

The title includes any legal easements referred to in clause LR11.1 of the registered lease but is subject to any rights that are granted or reserved by the lease and affect the registered land.
